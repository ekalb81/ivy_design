Competitive Analysis

Regarding checkout processes, some companies have already done a lot of problem-solving to reduce bounce rates and raise conversion rates. I think it’s important to take a look at how other websites have approached this problem.

I looked at three companies and their respective websites: REI, Canyon, and Trek Bikes

Design

Low-Fidelity Prototype

I took the red route and developed the first low-fidelity prototype. Due to the time constraints, I chose to sketch the prototype and test it in person. 

The candidates I chose had to be interested in biking in some capacity, but I wanted them to have varying degrees of online mobile shopping experience so that I could develop edge cases as early as possible.

The candidates were chosen and the interviews were conducted via in-person. The candidates gave valuable feedback at this stage of development.

User Testing

I conducted 5 in-person usability tests. I ended up developing a second red route that would become a second task the user had to complete during the tests.

  1. You are in the market for a mountain bike. How would you decide between two bikes? 
  2. Purchase the bike of your choice. 

I began by introducing myself and asking them some preliminary questions to get them comfortable. I helped them become familiar with the homepage of the website and told them about our goals. I proceeded to give them the tasks while encouraging them to think out loud. The test was conducted on 5 different users. The testers were recruited from known contacts.

Findings

Issue #1 There was no filter to help users narrow down their search results.

Summary: Several users took some time to find a way to narrow their search results. They expressed the desire to look for particular features such as colors and sizes. This was further exasperated by the large number of results that their search produced.

Recommendations: An easy solution was to add a filter to the search results page. Another option would to be add more specified subcategories under each category in the dropdown menu. 

Issue #2 The pop-up confirmation to compare two items was redundant. 

Summary: One user pointed out that it seemed confusing to have to click a “compare” button twice to generate results. It also seemed to derail the flow of other testers when they got to this page. 

Recommendations: The solution for this was to eliminate the pop-up that had the user confirm the comparison a second time. The first button should simply generate the comparison for the user. There could also be a banner with the user’s chosen products at the bottom that is always visible. This would allow the user to compare products at any time. 

Issue #3 There was no order confirmation.

Summary: Users expressed the desire to see an order summary after they placed their order. They wanted some sort of notification that would alert them that a confirmation email was sent. Most users felt like the flow needed an additional page for the checkout process. 

Recommendations: An order summary needs to be added at the end of the checkout process. It should include the order number, and the order total, and notify the user that an email was sent to them with this information. There should also be a button that takes the user back to the homepage and the option for the user to create an account.

High-Fidelity Test Results

To recap, I tested users on two different tasks for the mobile website during the user interviews. I wanted to make sure the flows made sense to users and that I successfully implemented the comparison feature. The goal was to eliminate as many pain points as possible. 

  1. You are in the market for a mountain bike. How would you decide between two bikes? 
  2. Purchase the bike of your choice. 

For the high-fidelity prototype, I conducted the tests both in person and online via Zoom. I began by introducing myself and asking the users some preliminary questions to get them comfortable. I helped them become familiar with the homepage of the website and told them about our goals. I proceeded to give them the tasks while encouraging them to think out loud. Five rounds of user testing were conducted.

Findings

Issue #1 The users expressed a desire to have the ability to search for bikes right from the homepage.

Summary: When the users were asked to find a mountain bike, most of them initially looked for a search bar on the homepage instead of using the dropdown menu. They seemed confused as to why the homepage didn’t incorporate a search bar and reluctantly would click either the drop-down menu or the call-to-action button (with the latter being more frequent).

Recommendations: Adding a search bar to the home page was the obvious solution.

Issue #2 It was not immediately apparent that the check boxes were for the item comparison feature.

Summary: When going to compare an item, users wanted to click on each individual product to compare them. When the checkboxes and comparison feature were eventually explained, they said it was not immediately apparent.

Recommendations: For this issue, I believe adding a label next to the checkboxes will remedy the problem. Maybe some sort of animation feature highlighting the checkboxes when first navigating to the search results page could also be beneficial.

Issue #3 There was no indication of how much sales tax was added.

Summary: Users were confused about the total on the shopping bag page. When checking out, they asked why the total was different from the original product total. They wanted to know exactly how much tax was being added.

Recommendations: For this problem, there were a couple of solutions. For one, add a line showing how much the tax is on the bag page and checkout page. Alternatively, only show the subtotal of the products themselves in the bag, and add a line detailing the tax amount on the checkout page only. The latter may increase conversions due to the bag total being less than the checkout total.

Reflection & Next Steps

After doing one round of high-fidelity testing for the Highland Bikes mobile website and implementing the changes, another round of user testing would be the next step. Several more rounds of ideating and testing are necessary before launching these changes to the website. 

I believe that through competitive analysis, user testing, and iterating on the designs, the bounce rate at checkout will be lowered and the overall conversions will increase. Through the addition of the product comparison feature, I believe there will be a reduction in cognitive load for the user, and they will be able to come to a decision more quickly when shopping for a bike on the website. The design decisions for the comparison feature will make it easy to navigate across mobile devices. Overall, I believe this was a successful project.
